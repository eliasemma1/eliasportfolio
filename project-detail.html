Autonomous Ground Vehicle
(AGV) — Competition Build

A fully custom-designed autonomous mobile robot built for the West African University Robotics Challenge. The system integrates a purpose-built chassis, differential drivetrain, ultrasonic sensor array, and Arduino Mega control unit into a cohesive, competition-ready platform.

01 The Problem

The challenge brief required a robot capable of autonomous navigation through a structured obstacle course — stopping at checkpoints, avoiding collisions, and completing the route within a 90-second time limit.

The core engineering problem: build a robot that is mechanically reliable, electronically responsive, and light enough to move quickly — all within a strict bill of materials budget and without access to expensive off-the-shelf chassis solutions.

Most teams purchased pre-built chassis kits. We chose to design everything from scratch, which gave us full control over geometry, sensor placement, centre of mass, and repairability during the competition.

02 Design Process

The project followed a structured engineering design cycle: requirements definition → concept sketching → CAD modelling → manufacturing → assembly → testing → iteration.

Concept & Sketching

Early-stage concept sketches explored three chassis configurations: 4-wheel differential, 3-wheel with central pivot, and a 6-wheel skid-steer layout. The 4-wheel differential was selected for its predictable kinematics and mechanical simplicity.

CAD Modelling — SolidWorks

The full assembly was modelled in SolidWorks 2023. The chassis consisted of 17 unique parts: aluminium frame extrusions, custom motor mounts, wheel hubs, sensor brackets, and an electronics tray. All parts were designed for FDM 3D printing or laser-cut aluminium sheet.

The design used a parametric approach: key dimensions (wheelbase, track width, ground clearance) were set as global variables so the whole model could be scaled or adjusted without individual part edits.

Electronics & Firmware

The control stack ran on an Arduino Mega 2560 with 5 HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensors (front, front-left, front-right, rear-left, rear-right) feeding a simple reactive obstacle-avoidance algorithm written in C++. Two N20 geared DC motors were driven via an L298N dual H-bridge motor driver.

A secondary IR line-following array allowed checkpoint detection on the competition floor markings. The firmware used a finite state machine (FSM) architecture: LINE_FOLLOW → OBSTACLE_AVOID → CHECKPOINT_STOP states.

Design to Competition

The robot successfully completed all 3 qualifying runs without collision, placing 2nd overall out of 14 competing teams. The custom chassis outperformed several kit-based platforms in manoeuvrability and proved significantly easier to repair between runs due to modular part design.

Lessons Learned

Parametric CAD saves time under pressure. Having global variables meant we could resize the wheelbase in 20 minutes when the competition surface turned out to be narrower than expected.

Sensor placement is a mechanical problem. The front sensor geometry created a blind spot in early tests — a physical problem solved by redesigning the mounting bracket angle, not the code.

Centre of mass matters at competition speeds. An early iteration with top-heavy electronics tray caused tipping on sharp turns. Moving the battery pack lower cut tipping incidents to zero.

Document as you build, not after. Retroactive assembly documentation consumed nearly 6 hours. Future projects will follow parallel design-documentation workflow.
